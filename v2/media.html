Blockchain technology first grew popular as the backbone of Bitcoin, the world’s first and largest cryptocurrency. After its emergence, people often confused blockchain with Bitcoin, rather than seeing it as the technology that created the cryptocurrency. But, soon, the world started seeing it for what it was; a technology that had the potential to disrupt the business model of industries across the world.

Although India missed the initial dotcom wave, it caught on to the blockchain and cryptocurrency wave right on time. In 2016, blockchain technology saw a lot of traction in the banking, finance and insurance industry, with companies incorporating this technology into their daily processes. When the Supreme Court of India in 2020 overturned the ban on crypto assets in the country — imposed by the Reserve Bank of India back in 2018 — many industry leaders and startups used this opportunity to venture into the crypto world.

Indeed, blockchain technology is still in its nascent stages in India, but industries beyond finance, banking, and insurance are now actively using distributed ledger systems for multiple use cases. According to a report, 56% of Indian industries are inclined to make blockchain a part of their core operations, and it does not just include private players but the public sector too, which uses this technology for use cases such as vehicle lifecycle management, electronic health record management, and farm insurance.

The cryptocurrency revolution in India

India’s journey to becoming one of the largest contributors to the crypto market began even before the Supreme Court of India lifted the ban on crypto assets in 2020. In 2019, Polygon (formerly Matic Network), a protocol and framework for producing and connecting Ethereum-compatible blockchain networks, became one of India’s first startups that launched its Token Sale on the Binance Launchpad IEO and raised $5,600,000. After the ban was lifted in 2020, the crypto revolution took the Indian market by storm. Chainalysis, a blockchain data platform in its 2021 Global Crypto Adoption Index, ranked India second in terms of crypto adoption worldwide, signifying the growing adaptation of this technology in India.

In case of tokens, fans have access that doesn’t expire and isn’t limited to a particular season. What’s more is that when one is an ‘invested fan’, they have rights to influence club decisions, get VIP benefits such as merchandise discounts, meet and greets and much more, and also have the chance to get financial incentives as these tokens are tradable.

Cryptocurrency is on every eager investor’s tongue, and one can’t help but wonder how it works. When we speak of cryptocurrency, we essentially speak of ‘tokens’ and ‘coins’ — two points of value given to each purchasable item in a blockchain. While the terms ‘coin’ and ‘token’ often go hand in hand, they are two very distinct terms. ‘Coins’, being the most well-known mode of cryptocurrency transactions, work on independent blockchains. To break it down, ‘BitCoin’ runs on the Bitcoin blockchain and so on. Just like that, you can tell that coins are used directly for transactions, just like regular money. However, tokens run a little differently. While Coins work on independent blockchains, ‘tokens’ are applied on already existing blockchains. Tokens, however, come in the form of tradable digital assets.
